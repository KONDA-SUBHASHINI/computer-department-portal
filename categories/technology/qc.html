Quantum Computing

Introduction

Quantum computing uses quantum-mechanical phenomena — like superposition and entanglement — to process information. Qubits can hold more than one state at a time, allowing quantum machines to explore many possibilities simultaneously. This unlocks huge speedups for problems in cryptography, simulation, optimization and material design.

Today quantum hardware is still maturing (decoherence, error rates and cooling requirements are challenging), but progress in algorithms and engineering is accelerating practical use-cases.

Key concepts & characteristics:

⚛ Superposition

Qubits can represent multiple values at once, enabling parallel exploration of possibilities.

⚛ Entanglement

Entangled qubits share correlated states — manipulating one affects the others instantly.

⚛ Quantum Gates

Operations (gates) rotate and interfere quantum states — basic building blocks for quantum programs.

⚛ Quantum Algorithms

Algorithms like Shor’s and Grover’s show quantum advantage for factoring and unstructured search.

⚛ Limitations

Current devices need extreme cooling, suffer noise and require error correction to be fully practical.
